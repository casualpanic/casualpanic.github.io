## nyneetal, a palimpsest

scholars have conceptualised a palimsest as referring to a place that is a patchwork of many interpretations. how one sees a place depends on who they are and where they come from. to a geologist doing fieldwork in the kumaon himalaya, the moutnain is a site of scientific research. the earth they'd dig into would constitute the raw materials for their scientific discoveries. they might produce a map of the geological section of the kumaon mountains, full of various visual language that brings to light certain aspects of that environment that the geologist wants you to see. for example, in the 19th century, east india company employee, the surgeon-physician john mcclelland produced a vertical section of the kumaon himalaya. in it, he used various visual cues to tell us about the layout of the region, using upward and downward markers to show actual movement across the toppgraphy that formed the subject of his study (Figure 1). in the above map, various elements come to light. at the bottom is a beatifuly complex illustration that takes the observer from low-lying, miasmic 'terai' region, to the picturesque villages in the foothills of the himalaya, viz. almora and pithoragarh (where the English had established rudimentary military barracks during, and following, the anglo gurkha war (1816)), all the way to the "uninhabited and inaccessible mountains," which he did indeed cross, being a swashuckling east india officer at the very frontiers and edges of western knowledge, quite literally, and mapping out this region of the Himalaya for the first time in such great scientific depth for keen audiences in the metropolis interested in its further examination.  The word terai itself mcclelland argues comes from the persian term 'moist', which, insists "is used in this instance by the natives of India, to designate the low belt of forest that extends along the base of the Himalaya for several hundred miles (page 25).
